Stress Management Knowledge Organiser

What is Stress?

  • Natural physical and emotional response to events or thoughts that impact wellbeing Example: Feeling nervous before a job interview, which may cause increased heart rate and sweating.
  • Can be caused by internal or external sources Internal source: Self-doubt about your abilities. External source: Unexpected work deadline.

Symptoms of Stress

  1. Physical:
    • Increased heart rate Your heart beats faster than normal, which you might notice especially when trying to relax.
    • Sweating You might experience excessive sweating, particularly in your palms or underarms.
    • Tension in body You might feel tightness in your muscles, especially in your neck, shoulders, or back.
  2. Emotional:
    • Anxiety Feeling worried or uneasy about future events or current situations.
    • Anger Feeling irritable or having a short temper, even over minor issues.
    • Depression Feeling sad, hopeless, or losing interest in activities you usually enjoy.
  3. Behavioural:
    • Difficulty concentrating Finding it hard to focus on tasks or remember important information.
    • Aggressive behaviour Lashing out verbally or physically at others when you normally wouldn't.
    • Changes in eating habits Eating much more or much less than usual, or craving unhealthy foods.

Sources of Stress

  • Internal: Thoughts, feelings, beliefs Example: Believing you're not good enough, or constantly worrying about the future.
  • External: Events, situations Example: Losing a job, moving to a new city, or experiencing a natural disaster.

Potential Sources:

  • Health issues Chronic illnesses, injuries, or concerns about future health problems can be significant sources of stress.
  • Relationship problems Conflicts with family, friends, or romantic partners can cause ongoing stress in daily life.
  • Work/study pressures Deadlines, exams, difficult coworkers or classmates, and heavy workloads can all contribute to stress.
  • Significant life changes Events like marriage, divorce, having a child, or retiring can be stressful even if they're positive changes.
  • Technology Social media pressures, cyberbullying, or the expectation to always be available can create stress.

Managing Stress: External Changes

  1. Time Management: - Set clear, achievable goals for your day or week
    - Use a planner or digital calendar to organize tasks
    - Break large projects into smaller, manageable steps
    • Set goals
    • Prioritize tasks
    • Use to-do lists
  2. Problem Solving: - Brainstorming: Write down all possible solutions without judging them
    - IDEAL method: Identify the problem, Define it clearly, Explore solutions, Act on the best one, Look back and evaluate
    - Pros and cons: List advantages and disadvantages of each possible solution
    • Brainstorming
    • IDEAL method
    • Pros and cons analysis
  3. Talking to Others: - Share your feelings with a trusted friend or family member
    - Seek professional help from a counselor or therapist
    - Use helplines for immediate support in crisis situations
    • Friends/family
    • Counsellors
    • Support hotlines
  4. Other Strategies:
    • Learn assertiveness skills Practice expressing your needs and boundaries clearly and respectfully, without aggression or passivity.
    • Personal development training Attend workshops or courses to improve skills like public speaking, conflict resolution, or time management.
    • Maintain a balanced diet Eat regular, nutritious meals with plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.
    • Regular physical exercise Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate exercise most days of the week, like brisk walking, cycling, or swimming.
    • Practice relaxation techniques Try deep breathing exercises, progressive muscle relaxation, meditation, or yoga to calm your mind and body.

Managing Stress: Internal Changes

  1. Use Humour: Watch a funny movie, share jokes with friends, or try to find the amusing side of a stressful situation (when appropriate).
    • See the funny side of situations
    • Laugh at yourself
  2. Prioritise: Make a list of what's truly important to you in life (e.g., family, health, career) and focus your energy on these areas.
    • Identify what's most important to you
  3. Choose Not to React: When faced with a stressful situation, take a deep breath and count to ten before responding. This gives you time to think rationally.
    • Practice self-control
    • Respond calmly instead of reacting emotionally
  4. Change Focus: When facing a challenge, try to identify at least one positive aspect or potential opportunity within the situation.
    • Shift attention to positives
  5. Adjust Expectations: - Set achievable goals based on your current situation and abilities
    - Be willing to adapt your plans when circumstances change unexpectedly
    • Set realistic goals
    • Be flexible when circumstances change
  6. Accept Change: Practice mindfulness to stay present and accept current circumstances, rather than dwelling on how things "should" be.
    • Let go of resistance to new situations
  7. Consciously Change Your State of Mind: - Keep a journal to track your thoughts and emotions
    - Identify common triggers for negative thoughts
    - Use positive affirmations or visualizations to replace negative self-talk
    • Be aware of your feelings and thoughts
    • Identify triggers
    • Practice replacing negative thoughts with positive ones
